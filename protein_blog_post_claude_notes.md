# Protein Blog Post: Story Strategy Notes

## What This Article Is Actually About

The article argues that plant protein is abundant but poorly accessible to humans due to three layered biological problems in seeds (not leaves). Processing and food combinations close much of the gap. But methionine's role as a DIAAS-lowering "flaw" may be an inadvertent longevity advantage, making the goal strategic use of both plant and animal protein rather than making plant protein mimic animal protein.

---

## Key Story Decisions Made Through Editing

### The Rubisco Framing

**Original problem:** The article opened with Rubisco as an example of plant protein that's hard for humans to access. But Rubisco is actually excellent quality protein — complete amino acid profile rivaling dairy whey. The article's engineering section later presented Rubisco extraction as a promising commercial opportunity, creating a quiet internal contradiction.

**Pushback from Karin:** She flagged that Rubisco is in fact highly digestible and nutritionally excellent once extracted. The problem is not quality — it is extraction and concentration.

**Resolution:** Reframed from "Rubisco is inaccessible protein" to "Rubisco is excellent protein that is physically hard to get at." The intro now says its amino acid profile rivals dairy whey, which is why companies are working to commercialize it. This makes the engineering section a payoff (here is how they are solving the extraction problem) rather than a contradiction.

**Concrete number added:** A 100g serving of spinach delivers roughly half a gram of Rubisco and under 3g of total protein. Leaves are over 90% water. This makes the "dilution is the real problem" argument visceral rather than abstract.

### The Leaf vs. Seed Distinction

**Original problem:** The preamble used leaves (Rubisco, spinach) to illustrate why plant protein is hard to access. But the rest of the article — the DIAAS table, three layers, tofu, lentils — is almost entirely about seeds. The reader got a thorough explanation of why leaf protein is inaccessible and then the article pivoted to seeds without acknowledging the shift.

**Key biological point established in conversation:** Leaf proteins like Rubisco ARE complete and high quality. The amino acid incompleteness problem (layer 1 of the three layers) applies to seeds and grains, not to leaves. The Plant Problem section was corrected to reflect this: "Leaf proteins like Rubisco are actually complete... The problem is different for seeds, which is where almost all the plant protein people actually eat comes from."

**Resolution:** Added a seeds pivot paragraph to the preamble: "Most people trying to get protein from plants are not eating spinach for its Rubisco. They are eating lentils, chickpeas, tofu, and grain — seeds in various forms — and seeds are a different story."

### The Ruminant Comparison

**Original problem:** The ruminant paragraph was positioned at the end of the preamble, separated from the cellulose/leaf discussion by a paragraph about seed defenses. This made it feel like a third separate argument rather than the natural explanation for why leaf protein is specifically inaccessible to us.

**Resolution:** Moved the ruminant comparison next to the cellulose point so both the structural (cell wall) and systemic (wrong gut type) limitations are explained together. The argument now flows: leaves are dilute → protein is behind cell walls our gut cannot breach → ruminants solved this differently with microbial fermentation → our gut took a different route.

**Biological detail added about ruminants:** A cow is not primarily absorbing plant protein directly. The rumen microbes consume the plant material, multiply using its nitrogen, and the cow digests those microbes in later stomach chambers, harvesting high-quality microbial protein. It is less like eating grass and more like farming protein inside your own gut. This is qualitatively different from our system, not just quantitatively better.

**Why this matters for the article:** When a reader asks "if ruminants can get protein from leaves, why can't we just eat more spinach?" — the answer is not just volume but the fundamental difference in digestive strategy. We cannot replicate the rumen's efficiency by eating more.

### Preamble Structure and Promise

**Original problem:** The preamble set up the problem (plant protein is inaccessible) but closed with a topic statement rather than a promise. "That is what this article is built around" tells the reader what the article is about, not what they will be able to do or understand by the end.

**Resolution:** Rewrote the closing paragraph of the preamble as a promise: covers why seed-based plant proteins often fall short, how cooking/fermentation/food combinations change the picture, and why the amino acid shortfall may be a longevity advantage. This hints at the three practical payoffs and plants the seed for the methionine paradox without spoiling it.

### False Dichotomy in the Preamble

**Original problem:** "The question that matters is not which plant foods have the most protein but how much our bodies absorb" was a false dichotomy. The article very much cares about which plant foods have more protein — the whole DIAAS table and lentils vs. seitan comparison depends on it.

**Resolution:** "How much protein a plant food contains matters, but the gram count is only the starting point." Quantity matters AND absorption matters — the label only tells you the first.

## Style Preferences Established in This Session

- No em dashes. Use commas, periods, or restructure the sentence.
- Reduce colon and semicolon use. Lists separated by commas or written out as prose.
- No short punchy sentence fragments as paragraph openers ("The catch is one of scale." "Humans did not.")
- No teleological language ("designed to"). Use "evolved to" instead.
- Define jargon inline on first use: "healthspan (the period of life spent free of serious disease and functional decline)", "sarcopenia (age-related muscle loss)"
- NYT article style: flowing subordinate clauses, concrete examples, plain language
- Avoid AI slop transitions: "The catch is one of scale", "It is only appropriate that we start with", "This protein happens to be"
- Rhetorical questions should not be asked if the previous paragraph already answers them

## Factual Corrections Made

- Rubisco is in leaves (chloroplasts), not seeds. Seeds have different storage proteins.
- "Rice and beans" changed to "rice and lentils (and legumes more broadly)" for internal consistency with the DIAAS table.
- PDCAAS vs DIAAS: the Rice + Lentils combined score (0.71-0.77) comes from PDCAAS studies, not direct DIAAS measurement. These are different metrics.
- The 0.95-0.98 DIAAS claim for rice + lentils under optimized conditions was removed as false.
- Sprouting reduces phytate by 40-75% (not "retains roughly half" — the original confused retention with reduction).
- mTOR suppression does not cause sarcopenia. The original draft had this backwards. Sarcopenia in aging muscle is driven by chronic mTOR HYPERactivation from BCAA accumulation, not suppression.
- Low-phytate crops work by disrupting phytate biosynthesis pathways, not by reducing phytase inhibitor activity.
- Quinoa is a pseudocereal, not a grain.
- Vitamin C improves iron absorption by reducing Fe3+ to Fe2+ (more resistant to phytate chelation), not by competing with phytate for binding sites.
- Chicken absorption math: 30g protein at 95-99% digestibility yields 28-30g, not 27-29g.

## Structural Decisions

- Seeds paragraph removed from preamble: the seeds/antinutrient material introduced in the preamble was redundant with the three-layers section. It was cut and the preamble hands off to DIAAS and the three-layers section directly.
- Ruminant comparison removed from a standalone paragraph: folded into the leaf/cellulose discussion where it belongs logically.
- Strategic combinations table split into two: amino acid complementation and antinutrient mitigation are different strategies and were separated visually.
- Individual variation caveat added in three places: intro, DIAAS table footnote, and mTOR disclaimer — not just at the end.

## What the Article Delivers (for future reference)

1. Why plant protein is hard to access: dilution in leaves, amino acid incompleteness in seeds, cell walls, antinutrients
2. How to improve it: food combinations (amino acid complementation), cooking/soaking/sprouting (antinutrient mitigation), processing (tofu, tempeh)
3. The surprising longevity angle: methionine's role in mTOR and aging means plant protein's "flaw" may be an advantage
4. The pulsed activation framework: use animal protein around training, plant protein as the baseline
5. Rubisco as both problem (dilute in leaves) and future solution (commercial extraction underway)
